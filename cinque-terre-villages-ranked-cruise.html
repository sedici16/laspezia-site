Cinque Terre Villages Ranked for Cruise Passengers: Where to Go First

Cinque Terre is five villages, one train line, and a limited number of hours in port. The most common mistake cruise passengers make is trying to see all five â€” arriving back at the ship having spent most of their day on trains and platforms, eating a rushed lunch, and seeing each village only briefly. The better approach is to choose one or two villages and experience them properly.

This ranking is written specifically for cruise passengers with restricted time ashore. Each village is assessed on transit time from La Spezia, crowd levels, what you can actually do there in a few hours, and the quality of the overall experience. The ranking reflects the practical reality of visiting on a port day, not the abstract merits of each place on its own terms.

1. Vernazza â€” Best Overall Choice

Vernazza is consistently ranked the most beautiful of the five Cinque Terre villages, and the judgment holds up under examination. The harbor is a perfect natural basin â€” small, almost entirely enclosed, with fishing boats bobbing on turquoise water and the medieval watchtower (Torre Doria) rising above the piazza at the water's edge. The layout of the town distributes visitors through its lanes and up its terraced streets in a way that makes even a crowded day feel manageable.

Train journey from La Spezia: approximately 20 minutes. Crowds: significant in peak season but better distributed than Riomaggiore. Food: several good restaurants with outdoor seating facing the harbor. Hiking: the path toward Corniglia (northward) and toward Manarola (southward) starts here and offers outstanding views. Time needed: two to three hours to do it properly.

The one weakness of Vernazza is that the main square floods with visitors when cruise ships are in port. Arriving early in the morning or later in the afternoon changes the experience significantly. If your ship docks early, Vernazza before noon is one of the genuinely beautiful experiences this part of Italy offers.

2. Monterosso al Mare â€” Best for a Full Beach Day

Monterosso is the largest Cinque Terre village and the only one with a proper sandy beach â€” a legitimate factor if you want to swim. It's divided into old and new sections, with the historic old town worth exploring independently of the beach. The Church of San Giovanni Battista in the old town has a fine Gothic interior; the 14th-century aurora tower above the beach is accessible by a short walk.

Train journey from La Spezia: approximately 25 minutes. Crowds: substantial at the beach in peak season. Food: excellent â€” the anchovies caught and processed locally are arguably the best food product in Cinque Terre. Order them in every form the restaurant offers. Time needed: two to three hours minimum for beach and town together.

Monterosso is the right choice if you want to combine sightseeing with actual swimming. It's less dramatically picturesque than Vernazza in terms of pure visual impact, but it offers a more complete day. See our guide to beaches near La Spezia for how it compares against beaches elsewhere in the region.

3. Manarola â€” Best for Photography

Manarola produces the signature image of Cinque Terre â€” the cluster of tall, narrow buildings in ochre and terracotta above the small harbor, best photographed from the path above the village. That view is real and it's extraordinary, particularly in the golden hour before sunset or in early morning light.

Train journey from La Spezia: approximately 12 minutes. Crowds: heavy â€” the path above the village is narrow and becomes congested quickly. Food: fewer options than Vernazza or Monterosso. Activities: beyond the harbor view and a walk through the village lanes, limited. Time needed: 60 to 90 minutes is sufficient.

Manarola works well as a short stop combined with another village rather than as a destination in itself. Take the train from La Spezia, photograph the harbor view, have a coffee in the village, and then continue to Vernazza for the remainder of your time ashore.

4. Riomaggiore â€” Best If Time Is Tight

Riomaggiore is the closest Cinque Terre village to La Spezia â€” under 10 minutes on the train. This proximity makes it the natural first stop for cruise passengers with limited time, and the village itself is genuinely attractive: a deep ravine of colorful buildings descending to a small harbor, with a walk along the cliff top offering views back over the coast.

Train journey from La Spezia: under 10 minutes. Crowds: very high â€” it's both close and well-known, and receives the full weight of cruise ship passenger traffic. Food: adequate but not distinctive. Activities: harbor walk, castle ruins above the village, the cliff path. Time needed: 60 to 90 minutes.

Riomaggiore's weakness is that its proximity to La Spezia makes it the first choice for the least engaged cruise passengers â€” the ones who get off the train, take a few photos, eat at the first restaurant they see, and get back on. The village deserves better, but the atmosphere on a busy day can be overwhelmingly tourist-oriented. If you're going here, go early.

5. Corniglia â€” Skip If Time Is Short

Corniglia is the only Cinque Terre village not at sea level. It sits on a headland 100 meters above the water, accessible from the train station by a staircase of 382 steps (the Lardarina) or by a shuttle bus that runs during busy periods. The village itself is smaller and quieter than the others, with fewer restaurants and no harbor. Swimming requires a 20-minute descent to the rocky beach below.

Train journey from La Spezia: approximately 17 minutes. Crowds: the lowest of the five villages due to the access barrier. Food: limited options. Activities: the views from the headland are genuinely outstanding, and the village has a calm that the others have lost. Time needed: 60 minutes plus descent time.

For a cruise passenger with five hours in port, Corniglia is the wrong choice. The step climb and relative scarcity of things to do compared to Vernazza or Manarola makes it a poor use of limited time. On a return visit, when you've seen the more obvious options, Corniglia is worth exploring for its tranquillity.

Recommended Itineraries for Cruise Passengers

Half-day (3â€“4 hours ashore)

Take the early train to Vernazza. Walk the harbor and the village lanes. Eat lunch at a restaurant with a harbor view. Return to La Spezia by 1pm. Alternatively, combine Manarola (45 minutes) and Riomaggiore (90 minutes) for two villages in a half day.

Full day (6+ hours ashore)

Train to Monterosso for a morning at the beach and the old town. Train to Vernazza for lunch and afternoon exploration. Return to La Spezia by late afternoon.

When the Villages Are Not the Right Choice

There are days when Cinque Terre is simply too crowded for a comfortable experience. Peak summer weekends, days when multiple large cruise ships are in port simultaneously, and Italian public holidays all produce conditions where the narrow lanes and limited restaurants cannot absorb the volume of visitors without significant discomfort.

On those days, the alternatives available from La Spezia are genuinely strong. Porto Venere, Lerici, and the Ligurian hills offer the same coastal Italian atmosphere without the congestion. For cruise passengers who want something completely different from coastal sightseeing, the truffle hunt from La Spezia takes you inland into the forests of the Ligurian Apennines for an experience that has nothing to do with crowds and everything to do with the deeper food culture of this region.
