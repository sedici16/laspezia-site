Lucca from La Spezia Cruise Port: The Walled City Worth Every Minute

Most cruise passengers at La Spezia head to the coast. That's entirely understandable â€” Cinque Terre and Porto Venere are extraordinary. But the region rewards those willing to look inland. An hour by train from La Spezia sits Lucca: a perfectly preserved Renaissance walled city in the Tuscan plain, and one of Italy's most underrated destinations.

Lucca has a quality that's increasingly rare in Italian tourism: it feels like a real place where people actually live. The walls still stand, forty metres wide in places, and you can walk or cycle the three-kilometre circuit at the top. The piazzas are genuinely used by locals. The restaurants serve food that reflects the regional tradition rather than menus optimized for international tourists.

Getting from La Spezia to Lucca

Regional trains run between La Spezia Centrale and Lucca roughly every hour. The journey takes between 55 and 75 minutes depending on whether the service is direct or requires a change at Viareggio. Check the Trenitalia schedule before you travel â€” the direct services are preferable for cruise passengers managing tight schedules.

Lucca train station sits just outside the city walls on the northern side. From the station, it's a two-minute walk through the gate and you're in the old city. No transfer, no taxi queue, no navigation needed. That simplicity is part of what makes Lucca such a good independent day trip.

A round trip from La Spezia to Lucca and back typically takes about two and a half hours in transit. Add three to four hours in Lucca itself and you're looking at a six to seven hour day trip â€” manageable if your ship is docked for a full day, but tight if you have an early all-aboard time.

The City Walls

Lucca's walls are the defining feature of the city â€” a 4.2-kilometre circuit of Renaissance fortifications built in the 16th and 17th centuries, wide enough at the top to accommodate a tree-lined promenade used daily by joggers, cyclists, and families. You can walk the entire circuit in about an hour, or rent a bicycle from one of the many rental shops near the gates and cycle it in 20 to 30 minutes.

The walls offer views into the old city on one side and out over the Tuscan plain on the other. Walking even a section gives you an immediate sense of Lucca's layout and sets the city in its landscape in a way that map reading never quite achieves.

Piazza dell'Anfiteatro

This is the most unusual piazza in Italy. The oval shape traces the outline of a Roman amphitheatre that once occupied the space â€” the medieval buildings that grew on the ancient foundations preserved the elliptical form over centuries. Today the piazza is lined with restaurants and cafes with outdoor seating. It fills with locals in the evening, but even in the afternoon it has a distinct, unhurried character worth experiencing.

Piazza San Michele and the Cathedral

The Romanesque facade of the Church of San Michele in Foro is one of the most elaborate in Tuscany â€” a stacked loggia of decorative arches and columns rising above the main square. The Cathedral of San Martino, a 10-minute walk away, houses Jacopo della Quercia's tomb of Ilaria del Carretto â€” one of the most beautiful marble sculptures of the Italian Renaissance, and one that most visitors have never seen.

Torre Guinigi and the Rooftop Trees

The Torre Guinigi is a 14th-century tower topped, improbably, by a garden of holm oak trees. Seven trees, their roots embedded in soil at the tower's summit, have grown here for centuries. Climbing the 230 steps is rewarded with panoramic views over the city and the unmistakeable sight of full-grown trees growing from a medieval tower.

Where to Eat in Lucca

Lucca is in Lucchese cooking territory â€” a sub-tradition of Tuscan cuisine with its own distinctive character. Buccellato is the traditional sweet bread of Lucca, flavoured with anise and raisins, sold at the bakery Amedeo Giusti near Piazza San Michele that has been making it since 1881. For lunch, the trattorie on the streets behind the main piazzas offer better food at lower prices than the tourist-facing restaurants on the main squares. Look for handwritten daily specials â€” ribollita, pappardelle with hare ragu, roasted meats.

Lucca vs Pisa: Which Inland Day Trip Is Worth It?

The honest answer is that for most visitors, Lucca is the better choice. The Leaning Tower of Pisa is genuinely iconic, but the experience of visiting it is one of Italy's most commercially overwhelming. Lucca has no single landmark with the name recognition of the Leaning Tower, but the city as a whole is more rewarding, more comfortable, and more likely to give you a memory of Italy that feels authentic.
